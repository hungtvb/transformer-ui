# PROJECT TITAN — EMOTION CURVE

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the intended emotional progression of PROJECT TITAN. It aligns story, gameplay, camera, motion, art, sound, interface behavior, pacing, and future branching around a single player experience.

The project is not built around combat intensity. Its emotional engine is uncertainty becoming recognition.

---

## 2. Emotional Thesis

The player begins alone with a dead machine and ends in a moment of mutual acknowledgement with an unknown intelligence.

The desired progression is:

**Disorientation → Agency → Curiosity → Suspicion → Awe → Vulnerability → Trust → Consequence**

The experience should feel earned. Awe must not arrive before the player has invested attention and physical effort.

---

## 3. Emotional Principles

### 3.1 The player acts before the story explains

Emotion grows from participation. The player restores, tunes, tracks, waits, and reaches before receiving clear answers.

### 3.2 Tension comes from uncertainty, not punishment

The player should worry about what the signal means, not about losing progress to arbitrary failure.

### 3.3 Silence is an active tool

Moments of reduced sound and motion create space for interpretation and emotional ownership.

### 3.4 Intimacy follows scale

The experience first establishes orbital distance and machinery, then narrows to one hand against one pane of glass.

### 3.5 The entity must remain partially unknowable

Connection should not eliminate mystery. The player understands intent before identity.

### 3.6 Emotional clarity matters more than plot density

One readable emotional beat is more valuable than several simultaneous narrative facts.

---

## 4. Target Curve Overview

| Phase | Primary Emotion | Secondary Emotion | Intensity | Player Need |
|---|---|---|---:|---|
| Entry | Disorientation | Isolation | 2/10 | Orientation |
| Calibration | Concentration | Unease | 3/10 | Basic control |
| Power | Agency | Relief | 4/10 | Visible consequence |
| Signal | Curiosity | Suspicion | 5/10 | Pattern recognition |
| Radar | Tension | Competence | 6/10 | Confirmation |
| First sight | Awe | Fear | 8/10 | Time to observe |
| Approach | Vulnerability | Anticipation | 8/10 | Consent and control |
| Hand contact | Intimacy | Uncertainty | 9/10 | Mutual response |
| Acknowledgement | Trust | Wonder | 7/10 | Meaningful confirmation |
| Future choice | Responsibility | Doubt | 8/10 | Consequence ownership |
| Ending | Reflection | Hope or regret | 5/10 | Emotional closure |

Intensity should rise in waves, not as one uninterrupted ramp.

---

## 5. Beat-by-Beat Emotional Design

## Beat 0 — Arrival in Darkness

### Intended emotion

Disorientation, isolation, and cautious attention.

### Player thought

“Where am I, and is anything still functioning?”

### Design treatment

- near-dark environment;
- minimal interface;
- distant structural ambience;
- no immediate exposition;
- one readable calibration objective;
- no threat indicator.

### Avoid

- dramatic alarm on entry;
- lore dump;
- full station reveal;
- immediate entity tease;
- frantic camera motion.

### Exit condition

The player understands how to look, focus, and interact.

---

## Beat 1 — Calibration

### Intended emotion

Concentration with a trace of unease.

### Player thought

“This system is responding to me.”

### Design treatment

- precise, low-risk input;
- immediate mechanical feedback;
- restrained confirmation tone;
- subtle environmental response;
- stable camera behavior.

### Emotional function

Calibration creates ownership. The station stops being scenery and becomes an instrument.

### Exit condition

The player feels competent enough to attempt restoration.

---

## Beat 2 — Power Restoration

### Intended emotion

Agency, relief, and the first reward.

### Player thought

“I brought part of this place back.”

### Design treatment

- physical lever resistance;
- staged power return;
- lights waking by zone;
- low-frequency machinery response;
- increased environmental readability;
- no immediate danger penalty.

### Emotional shape

Build effort during the lever action, then release it through a visible station-wide response.

### Avoid

- instant full illumination;
- explosive effects;
- exaggerated success celebration;
- every screen activating at once.

---

## Beat 3 — Signal in Static

### Intended emotion

Curiosity becoming suspicion.

### Player thought

“This is not random noise.”

### Design treatment

- incomplete pattern fragments;
- tuning action with layered feedback;
- brief moments of near-recognition;
- static that becomes structured;
- first subtle use of entity-associated color;
- system language that remains technical.

### Emotional rhythm

The player repeatedly approaches clarity, loses it, then finally locks the carrier.

### Avoid

- readable alien message immediately;
- obvious voice dialogue;
- loud horror sting;
- explicit identification.

### Exit condition

The player believes an intentional source exists.

---

## Beat 4 — Radar Tracking

### Intended emotion

Tension, competence, and confirmation.

### Player thought

“It is moving—and it may be reacting to me.”

### Design treatment

- moving target requiring attention;
- readable tracking tolerance;
- increasing coherence as lock improves;
- motion that suggests intent rather than simple drift;
- moderate time pressure without harsh failure;
- stronger station response at confirmation.

### Emotional function

Radar converts abstract mystery into spatial presence.

### Tension rule

The challenge should focus attention. It must not become frustrating enough to break curiosity.

### Exit condition

The player accepts that a deliberate presence is approaching the relay.

---

## Beat 5 — First Visual Contact

### Intended emotion

Awe mixed with fear.

### Player thought

“It is real.”

### Design treatment

- short visual quiet before reveal;
- exterior space and station scale clearly framed;
- entity first shown as silhouette or partial form;
- minimal instructional UI;
- slower camera and animation tempo;
- soundscape opens, then thins;
- no immediate action demand.

### Hold duration

Allow enough time for the player to look and form an interpretation before advancing.

### Avoid

- jump scare timing;
- rapid approach;
- full-body close-up;
- descriptive narration;
- achievement pop-up over the reveal.

### Exit condition

The player has emotionally registered the entity, not merely seen it.

---

## Beat 6 — The Approach

### Intended emotion

Vulnerability, anticipation, and uncertainty about intent.

### Player thought

“Should I allow this to continue?”

### Design treatment

- entity closes distance slowly;
- station reflections become more prominent;
- the player retains control of progression;
- subtle interface instability mirrors proximity;
- motion remains deliberate and non-aggressive;
- environmental sound reduces as distance closes.

### Consent principle

The player should perform or confirm the action that permits intimate contact. The scene must not simply force the hand moment after a cinematic takeover.

### Avoid

- aggressive lunging;
- automatic camera lock that removes agency too early;
- obvious threat music;
- excessive shaking or alarm effects.

---

## Beat 7 — Hand Synchronization

### Intended emotion

Intimacy, vulnerability, and deep uncertainty.

### Player thought

“It is waiting for me.”

### Design treatment

- clear contact pad or hand-alignment interaction;
- sustained hold rather than quick click;
- entity mirrors or responds to player timing;
- visible glass thickness and separation;
- localized contact effect;
- reduced interface noise;
- quiet enough to perceive subtle biological and mechanical responses.

### Emotional peak

The peak is not the instant of touch. It is the sustained interval in which neither side withdraws.

### Timing rule

Do not rush confirmation. A short period of unresolved contact is essential.

### Failure treatment

Releasing early pauses or softly resets synchronization. It should not punish or shame the player.

### Avoid

- explosive energy burst;
- instant translation;
- melodramatic music climax;
- conventional handshake animation;
- entity expression that removes all ambiguity.

---

## Beat 8 — Acknowledgement

### Intended emotion

Trust, wonder, and emotional release.

### Player thought

“It understood that I chose to respond.”

### Design treatment

- player performs an explicit acknowledgement;
- entity responds with a distinct but restrained action;
- system state becomes coherent rather than chaotic;
- color systems briefly harmonize;
- sound resolves without becoming triumphant;
- narrative text, if any, remains minimal.

### Emotional function

This beat confirms mutual intent while preserving unanswered questions.

### Release rule

After the peak, intensity should fall enough for reflection. Do not immediately introduce another major threat.

---

## Beat 9 — Future Intent Analysis

### Intended emotion

Concern, responsibility, and intellectual tension.

### Player thought

“What is it asking me to do?”

### Design treatment

- conflicting interpretations;
- incomplete translation;
- evidence distributed across station systems;
- no obviously correct moral label;
- player action required to establish confidence.

### Narrative rule

The question shifts from “Is it alive?” to “What responsibility do I have toward it?”

---

## Beat 10 — Choice and Consequence

### Intended emotion

Responsibility, doubt, and commitment.

### Player thought

“This outcome belongs to me.”

### Candidate choices

- Trust: open or strengthen the bridge.
- Seal: protect the station and terminate contact.
- Transfer: accept a transformation or exchange whose cost is uncertain.

### Design treatment

- choices expressed through physical station operations;
- clear immediate consequences;
- ambiguous long-term implications;
- no morality meter;
- no last-second reversal that invalidates agency.

### Choice timing

The player must have time to review evidence and emotionally disengage from the entity before committing.

---

## Beat 11 — Ending and Echo

### Intended emotion

Reflection, hope, grief, wonder, or regret depending on choice.

### Player thought

“What did I begin—or prevent?”

### Design treatment

- return to a familiar station composition changed by consequence;
- use silence or a restrained final motif;
- show one clear visual result;
- preserve interpretive space;
- optionally acknowledge prior playthrough memory.

### Ending rule

The ending should answer the player's choice, not explain the entire universe.

---

## 6. Pacing Model

### 6.1 Breath pattern

Use a repeating structure:

1. orient;
2. build attention;
3. perform action;
4. receive consequence;
5. pause;
6. reinterpret.

Major reveals must include a pause before the next objective.

### 6.2 Approximate first-session duration

| Segment | Target duration |
|---|---:|
| Arrival and calibration | 10–25 seconds |
| Power restoration | 20–40 seconds |
| Signal tuning | 30–60 seconds |
| Radar tracking | 30–60 seconds |
| First visual contact | 25–45 seconds |
| Approach and hand synchronization | 40–80 seconds |
| Acknowledgement and reflection | 20–45 seconds |

The total current arc should generally land between three and six minutes depending on player pace and accessibility settings.

### 6.3 No-content gaps

A pause is intentional only when the player has something meaningful to observe, anticipate, or interpret.

Dead time without a readable emotional purpose should be removed.

---

## 7. Emotional Intensity Controls

### 7.1 Camera

- stable framing builds trust;
- slow movement builds anticipation;
- restrained push-in increases intimacy;
- sudden movement is reserved for real system disruption;
- loss of player camera control must be brief and motivated.

### 7.2 Motion

- mechanical systems use deliberate acceleration and settling;
- the entity uses low-frequency, organic timing;
- synchronization between player and entity motion signals trust;
- stillness before movement increases significance.

### 7.3 Lighting

- darkness supports uncertainty;
- staged illumination rewards agency;
- spectral entity light marks the unknown;
- shared color at contact marks temporary alignment;
- reduced flicker after acknowledgement signals stability.

### 7.4 Sound

- low station ambience establishes isolation;
- mechanical response reinforces control;
- signal texture supports curiosity;
- silence or reduced bandwidth supports awe;
- intimate detail supports contact;
- restrained harmonic resolution supports trust.

### 7.5 Interface

- dense technical information supports investigation;
- interface reduction supports emotional reveals;
- stable alignment signals confidence;
- interference signals proximity or uncertainty;
- confirmation should be precise, not celebratory.

---

## 8. Player Agency and Emotional Safety

### 8.1 No forced panic

The game should not use unavoidable countdowns, repeated failure alarms, or surprise punishment during first contact.

### 8.2 Respect hesitation

If the player pauses before contact, the entity should wait, observe, or subtly adapt rather than immediately withdrawing.

### 8.3 Recoverable interaction

Missing a tune, losing radar lock, or releasing the contact hold should create a readable recovery path.

### 8.4 Reduced-intensity mode

Accessibility options should allow:

- reduced flicker;
- reduced camera movement;
- reduced vibration;
- longer interaction windows;
- simplified radar behavior;
- automatic hold alternatives;
- lowered sound peaks;
- persistent prompts.

These changes must preserve the emotional sequence, even if mechanical intensity is reduced.

---

## 9. Emotional Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- using alarms for every transition;
- replacing mystery with random glitches;
- explaining the entity before contact;
- constant high-intensity music;
- making the entity obviously friendly or evil;
- punishing hesitation;
- skipping recovery after the emotional peak;
- placing achievements, tutorials, or menus over key reveals;
- turning contact into a power fantasy;
- adding dialogue that tells the player what to feel;
- introducing a villain immediately after acknowledgement;
- using cheap jump scares to create tension.

---

## 10. Replay Emotional Strategy

A repeat session should not simply reproduce the same emotional curve at lower impact.

Use subtle memory changes:

- faster recognition by station systems;
- altered calibration copy;
- a retained signal fragment;
- entity behavior suggesting recognition;
- changed timing at the hand moment;
- additional evidence before the final choice;
- environmental traces from prior outcomes.

The emotional focus of replay shifts from discovery to interpretation and consequence.

---

## 11. Validation Questions

During review or playtesting, ask:

- Did the player feel alone before feeling threatened?
- Did restoring power produce genuine agency?
- Did curiosity emerge before visual contact?
- Did radar tracking confirm intelligence rather than feel like a minigame?
- Was the first sight given enough time to land?
- Did the player feel they permitted the approach?
- Did the hand interaction feel intimate rather than mechanical?
- Was acknowledgement distinct from simple task completion?
- Did intensity fall enough afterward for reflection?
- Could players describe the entity's intent without claiming complete understanding?
- Did any UI, effect, or prompt interrupt a key emotional beat?
- Did accessibility settings preserve the same emotional order?

---

## 12. Playtest Signals

### Desired player behavior

- leaning closer during signal tuning;
- pausing after first sight;
- tracking the entity without immediately searching for instructions;
- hesitating meaningfully before contact;
- holding contact beyond the minimum requirement;
- verbally speculating about intent;
- remaining still during acknowledgement;
- reflecting on the choice after the scene ends.

### Warning signals

- rapid clicking through every stage;
- asking “what am I supposed to shoot?”;
- interpreting the entity as a jump scare;
- missing the first reveal due to interface clutter;
- frustration during radar or hold mechanics;
- laughter caused by exaggerated animation;
- assuming the final decision has an obvious correct answer;
- closing the experience immediately after acknowledgement without reflection.

---

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The emotional design is successful when:

- the experience moves clearly from isolation to mutual recognition;
- each gameplay mechanic supports its assigned emotion;
- tension is created without punitive failure;
- the first visual reveal produces a noticeable pause in most playtests;
- the player retains meaningful control during the approach;
- the contact moment is the emotional peak without relying on spectacle;
- acknowledgement creates release while preserving mystery;
- future choices feel like consequences of relationship, not menu selections;
- sound, camera, motion, art, and UI agree on the same intensity level;
- reduced-intensity settings maintain the narrative-emotional sequence;
- players can recount how their feelings changed, not only what tasks they completed.
