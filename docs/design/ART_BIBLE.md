# PROJECT TITAN — ART BIBLE

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the visual language of PROJECT TITAN. It exists to keep environment design, interface design, materials, lighting, effects, and promotional imagery coherent across every sprint.

The project must feel like a believable abandoned orbital relay that has been forced back to life for one impossible first-contact event.

The target is not generic science fiction. The target is restrained industrial realism with moments of awe.

---

## 2. Core Visual Thesis

PROJECT TITAN should look like:

- a station designed by engineers, not stylists;
- a place that once had a clear function;
- a machine that has survived beyond its intended lifespan;
- a hostile environment made temporarily intimate;
- a cold system interrupted by something alive.

The visual journey moves from darkness and uncertainty toward recognition and emotional connection.

---

## 3. Art Pillars

### 3.1 Function before decoration

Every visible object must imply a purpose. Panels, cables, vents, latches, displays, rails, shutters, conduits, and maintenance markings should feel connected to station operation.

Decorative geometry without function should be removed.

### 3.2 Age through use, not ruin

The station is neglected, not destroyed.

Preferred signs of age:

- worn contact points;
- dust in corners;
- faded labels;
- imperfect paint;
- surface oxidation;
- small replacement parts;
- cable repairs;
- inconsistent panel illumination.

Avoid excessive rubble, random sparks, fire, collapsed architecture, or post-apocalyptic clutter.

### 3.3 Controlled visual density

The world can be detailed, but interaction areas must remain readable.

Use visual hierarchy:

1. active objective;
2. interactable control;
3. system response;
4. environmental storytelling;
5. secondary decoration.

The player should never have to search through noise for the next meaningful action.

### 3.4 Contrast creates emotion

The station is rigid, geometric, cold, and mechanical.

The entity is organic, quiet, asymmetrical, and difficult to classify.

The strongest image in the experience is the meeting of those two visual systems.

### 3.5 Restraint preserves mystery

Do not explain the entity through excessive detail.

Its design should provide readable emotional intent while preserving uncertainty about biology, culture, and origin.

## 5. Color System

### 5.1 Base environment palette

The station uses desaturated industrial colors:

- charcoal black;
- graphite grey;
- oxidized steel;
- muted navy;
- dirty off-white;
- faded utility yellow;
- low-saturation emergency red.

Black should rarely be absolute. Dark surfaces should preserve subtle material separation.

### 5.2 Functional signal colors

Colors must carry consistent meaning.

- Cyan: stable system activity, navigation, alignment.
- Amber: incomplete task, caution, unstable operation.
- Red: fault, danger, denied state, overload.
- Green: confirmed lock, valid route, successful handshake.
- White: neutral information, labels, calibration.
- Violet or spectral blue: unknown entity influence only.

Entity-associated colors must never appear casually in ordinary station UI.

### 5.3 Color progression by act

#### Calibration

Near-black, low-contrast grey, sparse white calibration marks.

#### Power restoration

Amber utility lighting returns in sections.

#### Signal tuning

Cyan and white interface detail becomes more legible.

#### Radar tracking

Cool green and cyan dominate the tactical surface.

#### Visual contact

Station colors dim while exterior spectral tones become visible.

#### Hand synchronization

Warm skin-adjacent highlights and entity spectral color meet at the glass.

#### Acknowledgement

The palette reaches its most balanced state: machine cyan, human warmth, and entity violet coexist.

## 6. Lighting Bible

### 6.1 Lighting philosophy

Lighting is a narrative system, not decoration.

It communicates:

- available power;
- player progress;
- environmental scale;
- system stability;
- proximity of the entity;
- emotional temperature.

### 6.2 Sources must feel motivated

Primary sources:

- recessed utility strips;
- panel indicators;
- console displays;
- emergency lamps;
- exterior planetary or stellar light;
- entity-originated light;
- reflected light from the observation glass.

Avoid unexplained cinematic fill lights that make the station feel like a film set.

### 6.3 Darkness rules

Darkness should conceal information without making interaction impossible.

Requirements:

- active controls must remain readable;
- silhouette separation must survive on average displays;
- important labels need sufficient contrast;
- black areas must retain shape through reflection or edge light;
- darkness must not become muddy grey fog.

### 6.4 Entity lighting

The entity should not behave like a lamp.

Preferred effects:

- subtle edge response;
- spectral reflections on glass;
- low-frequency luminance changes;
- localized interference in nearby displays;
- faint light transfer at the contact point.

Avoid constant glowing eyes, full-body emissive materials, or aggressive pulsing.

---

## 7. Materials

### 7.1 Station materials

Primary materials:

- painted steel;
- anodized aluminum;
- rough polymer;
- reinforced observation glass;
- rubberized grips;
- ceramic electrical isolators;
- fabric or insulated cable sleeves.

### 7.2 Material storytelling

Wear should follow interaction and maintenance logic.

Examples:

- lever grip polished by repeated use;
- scratches near panel fasteners;
- fingerprints near diagnostic glass;
- dust removed around recently active vents;
- paint wear on floor paths;
- condensation near thermal boundaries.

### 7.3 Roughness discipline

Avoid making every surface equally glossy.

- painted metal: medium roughness;
- worn edges: slightly smoother;
- rubber grips: high roughness;
- glass: controlled reflection with visible thickness;
- displays: lower roughness but not mirror-like;
- entity surface: layered response, neither wet nor plastic.

### 7.4 Observation glass

The glass is a central character.

It must communicate:

- thickness;
- pressure resistance;
- distance between player and entity;
- reflection of the control room;
- distortion at oblique angles;
- physical contact when the hand arrives.

The glass should never become so reflective that the entity disappears.

---

## 8. Environment Design

### 8.1 Primary location

The core experience takes place in Orbital Relay 07's control and observation station.

The environment should suggest adjacent spaces without requiring exploration:

- maintenance corridors;
- antenna control systems;
- damaged relay infrastructure;
- dormant crew areas;
- external hull structures;
- deep orbital space beyond the station.

### 8.2 Spatial composition

The station should be readable as three layers:

1. foreground controls;
2. midground structural frame and glass;
3. background exterior space and contact target.

The player must always understand the relationship between control input and exterior consequence.

### 8.3 Scale cues

Use:

- heavy framing members;
- repeated structural modules;
- distant antenna geometry;
- slow exterior parallax;
- tiny maintenance lights;
- large glass thickness;
- sound-linked visual vibration;
- delayed system response.

Avoid oversized props that feel large only because the camera is close.

### 8.4 Environmental storytelling zones

Each visible zone should carry one quiet story.

Examples:

- an abandoned operator station;
- a manually repaired power junction;
- an old warning label from a previous incident;
- an inactive secondary chair;
- personal markings partially removed;
- a sealed maintenance hatch;
- a dead monitor showing the last valid timestamp.

Storytelling details must never compete with current gameplay.

---

## 9. Interface Art Direction

### 9.1 Interface philosophy

The UI should feel embedded in the station, not layered over the game.

Prefer diegetic and spatially anchored interfaces.

Use screen-space UI only for:

- accessibility;
- critical prompts;
- pause/settings;
- fallback instructions;
- system-level notifications.

### 9.2 Typography

Typography must feel engineered, compact, and legible.

Requirements:

- clear numeral distinction;
- high readability at small size;
- restrained use of uppercase;
- limited weight range;
- no decorative futuristic fonts;
- monospace reserved for telemetry and codes;
- proportional sans serif for instructions and narrative text.

### 9.3 Graphic language

Use:

- fine grid structures;
- alignment marks;
- reticles;
- signal graphs;
- segmented status bars;
- calibration ticks;
- technical labels;
- sparse warning blocks;
- controlled scan-line or phosphor effects.

Avoid:

- excessive hexagons;
- random rotating circles;
- fake code walls;
- meaningless data noise;
- constant glitch effects;
- large translucent HUD frames.

### 9.4 UI depth

Interfaces should exist at believable depths:

- printed labels on surfaces;
- emissive information beneath glass;
- projected or volumetric elements only when justified;
- reflections that respect screen position;
- display bloom kept subtle.

---

## 10. Entity Art Direction

### 10.1 Narrative function

The entity must support three simultaneous readings:

- potentially intelligent;
- potentially vulnerable;
- fundamentally unknown.

### 10.2 Silhouette

The silhouette should be readable at distance and remain visually distinct from a human.

It may imply familiar anatomy but must avoid becoming a person in makeup.

Preferred qualities:

- asymmetrical detail;
- elongated or unusual proportion;
- controlled stillness;
- a hand or contact structure capable of emotional clarity;
- layered surface forms that catch low light.

### 10.3 Face and gaze

The entity does not require a conventional human face.

Emotional connection may be communicated through:

- orientation;
- pause timing;
- proximity;
- breathing or surface movement;
- subtle focus behavior;
- response to the player's hand.

Avoid exaggerated facial animation or obvious smiles.

### 10.4 Surface treatment

The surface should feel biologically plausible but not wet, slimy, or grotesque.

Use:

- soft subsurface response;
- micro-variation;
- layered translucency;
- restrained spectral reflection;
- localized movement under the surface;
- subtle reaction near contact.

### 10.5 Reveal strategy

The entity is revealed in stages:

1. signal anomaly;
2. radar presence;
3. distant silhouette;
4. partial form;
5. approach;
6. contact structure or hand;
7. limited close detail.

Never show a clean full-body hero shot too early.

---

## 11. Effects

### 11.1 Permitted effects

- dust in directional light;
- subtle display persistence;
- restrained chromatic interference;
- localized electrical arcing during faults;
- soft condensation;
- signal noise tied to gameplay;
- glass contact distortion;
- minor camera-visible vibration;
- exterior particles for scale.

### 11.2 Effects to avoid

- constant sparks;
- smoke everywhere;
- extreme bloom;
- heavy lens flare;
- permanent screen shake;
- glitch overlays unrelated to system state;
- excessive film grain;
- rainbow chromatic aberration;
- large holographic particles.

### 11.3 Contact effect

The contact moment should be precise and intimate.

Recommended layers:

1. physical hand alignment;
2. slight glass compression or optical distortion;
3. localized condensation change;
4. minimal spectral light transfer;
5. station interface response;
6. silence or reduced visual noise.

Do not turn the moment into an explosion of energy.

---

## 12. Composition Rules

### 12.1 Active focus

Every scene should have one dominant focal point.

Secondary lights and motion should support that focus, not compete with it.

### 12.2 Framing the unknown

The entity should often be framed by station geometry, reflections, partial occlusion, or darkness.

This preserves scale and uncertainty.

### 12.3 Symmetry and disruption

The station may use strong engineered symmetry.

The entity should subtly disrupt that symmetry when it enters the frame.

### 12.4 Negative space

Use negative space to create anticipation, especially before visual contact.

Do not fill every dark region with detail.

---

## 13. Accessibility and Readability

Art direction must not obstruct interaction.

Requirements:

- never communicate critical state through color alone;
- use shape, label, motion, and sound together;
- maintain readable contrast for text and controls;
- avoid rapid flashes;
- provide reduced-effects alternatives;
- keep entity visibility sufficient during required tracking;
- preserve focus indicators for keyboard use;
- ensure prompts remain legible over complex backgrounds.

---

## 14. Performance-Aware Art Rules

Visual quality must scale gracefully.

Preferred techniques:

- reusable material families;
- atlased decals;
- limited transparent layers;
- LOD for exterior structures;
- bounded particle counts;
- baked or hybrid lighting where appropriate;
- effect quality tiers;
- texture resolution based on screen importance;
- simplified reflections on lower tiers.

Do not rely on one expensive effect to carry the entire visual identity.

---

## 15. Screenshot Moments

The project must intentionally support these key images:

1. The dead station before power restoration.
2. The first section of the control room waking up.
3. The signal resolving into an impossible pattern.
4. The radar target moving with intelligence.
5. The first distant silhouette beyond the glass.
6. The entity approaching through reflected station light.
7. The hand aligned against the observation window.
8. The acknowledgement response after contact.

Each moment should remain readable without explanatory text.

---

## 16. Review Checklist

For every new visual asset or scene, confirm:

- Does it support a real station function?
- Is the visual hierarchy clear?
- Does the material tell a believable history?
- Is the lighting motivated?
- Is the active interaction readable?
- Does the design preserve mystery?
- Does it avoid generic sci-fi decoration?
- Does it respect the color meaning system?
- Does it scale to lower performance tiers?
- Does it strengthen at least one screenshot moment?

---

## 17. Definition of Done

The art direction is successful when:

- the station feels coherent across all gameplay states;
- the player can read interactable objects without visual clutter;
- materials show believable age and use;
- lighting visibly tracks narrative progression;
- the entity feels emotionally legible but biologically unresolved;
- interface graphics feel native to the world;
- the contact scene remains intimate rather than spectacular;
- screenshots are recognizably PROJECT TITAN without relying on a logo;
- accessibility and performance constraints are respected;
- new contributors can make consistent visual decisions using this document alone.
