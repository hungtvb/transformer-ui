# PROJECT TITAN — Story Bible

> The browser is not your window. It is theirs.

## Purpose

This document defines the canonical narrative structure for PROJECT TITAN. It is the single source of truth for story order, reveal pacing, user role, emotional intent, world reactions, and narrative consequences.

PROJECT TITAN is not a character showcase. It is a first-contact experience told from inside an abandoned orbital relay station. The user does not arrive as a spectator. The user becomes the final operator capable of restoring the station, decoding an unknown signal, identifying a colossal machine, and deciding whether contact should continue.

## Narrative pillars

1. **Mystery before identity** — the entity is never named early.
2. **Presence before exposition** — the user feels the entity before understanding it.
3. **Scale through environment** — the room, window, radar, skyline, vibration, and sound establish size.
4. **Interaction as authorship** — the story only advances because the user acts.
5. **Trust is earned** — contact is built gradually through repeated physical exchanges.
6. **Silence creates meaning** — major reveals are preceded by reduced visual and audio activity.
7. **The entity remains ambiguous** — even when it appears peaceful, the user must decide what to believe.

## Player role

The user is the emergency operator of ORBITAL RELAY 07, a station left dormant after an unexplained communications blackout.

The station has no active crew. Its automated systems are degraded. Power, radar, signal processing, and defensive protocols require manual intervention.

The user is not given a biography. The experience should allow the user to project themselves into the role.

## World premise

A non-human machine intelligence has been attempting to reach Earth through damaged long-range channels. The station receives the transmission first.

The signal is incomplete. The visual feed is unstable. The object is massive enough to distort nearby sensors. The station cannot determine whether the contact is a visitor, survivor, weapon, or warning.

The entity appears to recognize deliberate human action.

## Story structure overview

| Act | Title | Primary emotion | Core user action | Story reward |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Calibration | Curiosity | Enter the station | The world becomes responsive |
| 1 | Station Awakening | Agency | Restore power | The station comes alive |
| 2 | Unknown Carrier | Mystery | Tune the signal | A fragmented voice appears |
| 3 | Moving Contact | Tension | Track the target | The object is confirmed alive |
| 4 | First Contact | Awe | Meet the hand at the glass | The entity acknowledges the user |
| 5 | Shared Signal | Connection | Stabilize communication | Meaning begins to emerge |
| 6 | The Warning | Fear | Interpret conflicting data | A second threat is revealed |
| 7 | The Choice | Responsibility | Route power or sever contact | The story branches |
| 8 | Consequence | Memory | Witness the result | The ending reflects the choice |

# ACT 0 — CALIBRATION

## Narrative objective

Convince the user that the browser is calibrating a real station rather than loading a website.

## User action

The user selects **ENTER STATION** after WebGL, input, and audio readiness are displayed.

## World reaction

- The black calibration space fades into the control room.
- Mechanical relays click in sequence.
- Emergency illumination activates at minimum power.
- The station reports that no autonomous recovery is available.

## Camera intent

Static but not lifeless. Use subtle breathing, sub-pixel sway, and slow focus drift.

## Audio intent

- Electrical hum.
- Isolated relay clicks.
- Very low sub-bass room tone.
- No music.

## Emotional target

Curiosity with mild unease.

## Acceptance criteria

- The user understands that the station is offline.
- No entity or silhouette is visible.
- The station feels older and more physical than a generic sci-fi HUD.

# ACT 1 — STATION AWAKENING

## Narrative objective

Give the user authorship over the world by making power restoration a physical action.

## User action

Drag the main power lever from 0% to full power.

## World reaction

Power should return in layers rather than all at once:

1. Emergency lamps flicker.
2. CRT panels warm up.
3. Radar begins a partial sweep.
4. Ventilation restarts.
5. Exterior shutters unlock.
6. A distant vibration appears in the structure.

## Camera intent

The camera remains inside the room and subtly settles as stabilization systems return.

## Audio intent

- Lever resistance.
- Transformer hum.
- Fans winding up.
- Intermittent static.

## Failure state

There is no hard fail. If the user releases early, the station partially powers down and waits.

## Emotional target

Agency and ownership.

## Signature moment

The room transitions from dead to alive because of the user.

# ACT 2 — UNKNOWN CARRIER

## Narrative objective

Introduce the first evidence of another intelligence without revealing form or identity.

## User action

Tune the analog frequency control until the carrier lock stabilizes.

## World reaction

As the user approaches the target frequency:

- Static narrows.
- The waveform becomes structured.
- A low mechanical vocal fragment becomes audible.
- Exterior lights pulse at the same rhythm.
- The room experiences a faint directional vibration.

## Canonical signal fragments

The message must remain incomplete. Suggested fragments:

- “...relay...”
- “...hear...”
- “...not alone...”
- “...behind...”

Do not reveal a name.

## Camera intent

Very slow movement toward the observation window, never enough to fully reveal the exterior.

## Emotional target

Mystery becoming tension.

## Transition condition

The story advances only after the signal remains above the lock threshold long enough to prove deliberate tuning.

---

# ACT 3 — MOVING CONTACT

## Narrative objective

Transform the unknown signal into a physical, moving presence.

## User action

Drag the radar reticle and maintain lock on a moving contact.

## World reaction

- The target changes speed and direction.
- Radar interference increases as the object approaches.
- Station lights react to proximity.
- Loose desk objects vibrate.
- The entity remains mostly hidden behind fog, darkness, and framing structures.

## Reveal strategy

The user may see only partial shapes:

1. A moving shadow across cloud.
2. A metallic edge outside the window.
3. Two separated light sources.
4. A shoulder-like silhouette crossing behind structural beams.

## Camera intent

The room remains the anchor. The target is seen through windows and instruments, not presented as a centered model.

## Emotional target

Focused tension.

## Success condition

Tracking quality reaches the lock threshold for a sustained duration.

---

# ACT 4 — FIRST CONTACT

## Narrative objective

Deliver the first major emotional payoff: the entity recognizes the user.

## User action

Place and hold a hand on the contact pad.

## World reaction

- The entity approaches the glass.
- Its hand aligns with the user’s input position.
- The window flexes under pressure.
- Fine condensation and dust move outward from the contact point.
- The entity’s eyes track the user.
- A handprint remains on the glass after synchronization.

## Important rule

The moment must not feel like a button triggering a canned animation. Contact strength, bloom, audio, room vibration, and the entity’s posture must respond continuously to hold progress.

## Camera intent

Keep the user inside the room. The hand and partial face should exceed the viewport, reinforcing scale.

## Audio intent

- Deep structural strain.
- Servo movement outside the glass.
- Audio narrows during synchronization.
- On completion, use one clear harmonic tone.

## Emotional target

Awe and connection.

## Screenshot moment

The user’s contact marker aligned with the entity’s hand on the other side of the glass.

---

# ACT 5 — SHARED SIGNAL

## Narrative objective

Prove that contact is intelligent and reciprocal.

## User action

Acknowledge the entity and stabilize a short pattern exchange.

## World reaction

- The entity deliberately mirrors a simple pulse pattern.
- The station translates fragments into concepts rather than full sentences.
- Eye movement and head position show attention.

## Canonical message concepts

- “You are here.”
- “The relay is unsafe.”
- “Something followed.”

## Emotional target

Trust mixed with uncertainty.

---

# ACT 6 — THE WARNING

## Narrative objective

Break the safety of the first-contact moment and introduce consequence.

## User action

Interpret conflicting radar and power data while the station destabilizes.

## World reaction

- A second signal appears.
- Defensive systems misidentify both contacts.
- The entity turns away from the glass for the first time.
- The room darkens as power demand rises.

## Reveal

The second contact is never fully shown. It should remain more threatening because it is incomplete.

## Emotional target

Fear and urgency.

---

# ACT 7 — THE CHOICE

## Narrative objective

Force the user to accept responsibility for what happens next.

## Canonical options

### Route power to the bridge

Continue contact and help the entity cross.

### Route power to station defense

Protect Earth and risk trapping the entity outside.

### Emergency sever

Destroy the link and lose all communication.

## Design rule

No option is presented as morally correct. The interface should communicate cost before confirmation.

## Emotional target

Responsibility.

---

# ACT 8 — CONSEQUENCE

## Ending A — TRUST

The entity crosses or secures the station. It acknowledges the user before departing toward the threat.

Final feeling: hope with uncertainty.

## Ending B — DEFENSE

The station survives, but contact is lost. The entity accepts the decision and withdraws.

Final feeling: safety with regret.

## Ending C — SEVER

The connection collapses. The room returns to silence. A final hidden signal implies the story is not over.

Final feeling: isolation and unresolved fear.

## Replay memory

The experience stores only lightweight local state:

- visit count,
- previous ending,
- whether first contact was completed,
- selected accessibility mode.

On later visits, the entity may acknowledge the user’s return, but no critical content may depend on local storage.

# Pacing rules

- Never reveal the entire entity in the first half.
- No two high-intensity moments should occur without a quieter recovery beat.
- Exposition must be delivered through systems, sound, reaction, and partial translation.
- Every act must contain one user-controlled change and one world-controlled surprise.
- The strongest visual moment must occur after the user has earned it.

# Canonical emotional curve

```text
Curiosity → Agency → Mystery → Tension → Awe → Connection → Fear → Responsibility → Memory
```

# Non-canonical content

The following are explicitly outside the main story unless approved in a future revision:

- full battle sequences,
- named licensed characters,
- superhero-style speeches,
- direct references to Transformers lore,
- generic “save the world” exposition,
- multiple unrelated robots appearing for spectacle.

# Story review checklist

A story change is acceptable only when:

- it preserves the slow reveal,
- it gives the user meaningful participation,
- it has a clear emotional purpose,
- it changes the world state,
- it respects the environment-first scale strategy,
- it does not rely on copyrighted character identity,
- it remains understandable without long text blocks.
